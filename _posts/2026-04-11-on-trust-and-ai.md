One of the central tensions in my research is this: we build AI tools to help people, but the act of helping can undermine the very capacities it's meant to support.

This isn't a new observation. It shows up in education research (does calculator use erode arithmetic intuition?), in GPS navigation (does turn-by-turn routing dull our spatial sense?), and now, acutely, in AI assistants that can write, reason, and coach on demand.

## What over-reliance actually looks like

Over-reliance isn't always obvious. It's rarely *I can't do this without the AI*. More often it's subtle: a shrinking sense of confidence, a growing habit of checking before deciding, a quiet atrophying of the skill you thought you were building.

In our work on Trucey — an AI negotiation coach — we found something interesting. Users who practiced with the AI felt *more* prepared going into negotiations on paper. But when we probed deeper, something was off: they were rehearsing the AI's strategies, not developing their own. The scaffolding had become the structure.

## The calibration problem

What we want isn't zero AI reliance — that's neither realistic nor useful. What we want is *appropriate* reliance: trusting the AI when it's more likely to be right than you, and trusting yourself when the reverse is true.

But calibration is hard. It requires knowing what you know, which is itself a metacognitive skill that AI tends to bypass rather than develop. When the AI answers before you've had time to form an opinion, the whole process short-circuits.

## A question I'm sitting with

Is there a form of AI assistance that makes people *more* capable over time, not just more productive in the moment? Or is that a category error — are these tools optimized for throughput, and we're wrong to expect them to be developmental?

I don't have a clean answer. But I think the question matters more than most of the benchmarks we run.
